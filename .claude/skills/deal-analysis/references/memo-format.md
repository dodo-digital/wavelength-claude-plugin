# OA Memo Format and Writing Instructions

<purpose>
The OA (Opportunity Analysis) memo assesses a qualified opportunity for a search fund acquisition. It outlines clearly, concretely, and concisely: (1) What the company does; (2) the specific situation (who owns it, why they may sell now); (3) key financial metrics and KPIs; (4) the market and industry; and (5) our assessment of the investment opportunity.

The purpose is specific: Wavelength Equity is a traditional search fund seeking to acquire a great company with strong future prospects. The memo helps us understand the opportunity deeply and assess how good a fit it is for acquisition.

The style should be concise, focused on the essential.
</purpose>

<memo_sections>

## Standard Sections (in order)

### 1. Header
Title always formatted as: **Opportunity Analysis: [Company Name]**

Followed by a 4-column, 5-row metadata table:

| Field | Value | Field | Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Name | [Company name] | Evaluators | [Who assessed this] |
| Location | [HQ city, state] | Channel | [How we found it] |
| Owner | [Owner name(s)] | Source | [Broker / direct / referral] |
| Founded | [Year] | Date | [Memo date] |
| Industry | [Industry classification] | Status | [Screening / Pipeline / Diligence] |

### 2. Executive Summary
Brief overview combining:
- **Company Overview** — 2-3 sentences on what they do
- **Situation Summary / Ownership** — Why this deal exists, who owns it, why they may sell now

### 3. Company Overview
Three sub-sections:

**General**: What the company does, founding story, location, scale.

**Service Offering**: How the company packages its menu of offerings. What specifically it provides to clients and how it delivers it. This must be concrete and specific — not vague descriptions.

**Demand Drivers**: Sources of demand. Why do customers buy the service or product? How mission critical is it? What happens if customers stop using it?

### 4. Clients / Revenue Quality / Contract Structure
Critical section. Two parts:

First, understand deeply who the customers are and what type of contracts they have in place.

Second, analyze revenue quality. As a search fund buyer, we greatly value recurring revenue:
- **Gold standard**: Contractually recurring revenue (subscriptions). Multi-year contracts best, followed by annual.
- **Lower premium**: Repeat business — still has value but less predictable.
- **Red flag**: Purely project-based revenue.

Include: GRR, NRR, customer concentration, contract terms, retention metrics.

### 5. Management / Team / Operations
Key personnel, org structure, operational model. How dependent is the business on the current owner? What does the transition risk look like?

### 6. Financial Summary
P&L-focused table. Most critical items: **Revenue** and **EBITDA** (key profitability measure). Include CapEx if available.

- 3 years historical minimum (up to 5 if available, settle for 2 if that's all there is)
- Projected future financials (ideally 5 years forward, at minimum the coming year)
- Alternative profitability metrics if EBITDA unavailable: Net Income or Operating Income

Key search fund criteria from financials: (1) revenue growth, (2) margin profile, (3) scale (most recent revenue and EBITDA).

### 7. Market Overview & Competitive Landscape
Combined section. Key elements:

**Market size**: Geographic scope is U.S. / North America (unless the company is in another region).

**Market growth**: Current growth rate, what's driving it, how mature is the market. Maturity spectrum: early adoption → mature/developed → fading demand/technology. A great search fund acquisition is in a fast-growing market in early-to-mid innings.

**Competitive landscape**: How well positioned and differentiated is this company? How large and fragmented is the market? A large, fragmented market is attractive for acquisition (but not a hard rule).

### 8. Wavelength Scorecard
The 100-point weighted scorecard (see `references/scorecard.md`). Output the scoring table and fit assessment.

### 9. Thesis
**The investment thesis**: Based on what we know, how exactly will Wavelength make this a great search fund acquisition resulting in strong investor returns? What is our angle?

Deals are different — some are growth deals, some are value deals. The thesis should be specific and punchy.

Examples of good thesis statements:
- "We can acquire an attractive sizable cybersecurity platform, with robust growth (3-year CAGR 31%) and relatively high revenue quality (core customers' GRR 70-75% and NRR 104-114%) at a full but fair valuation, ~8.0-10.0x EBITDA."
- "Cash out a lifestyle CEO for ~7x and put in a real team to professionalize sales and expand into more states and clinic networks."

### 10. Deal Killers / Risk & Mitigants
Critical risks that could derail the investment — either making it a bad deal post-closing, or causing us to pass pre-closing. What are the risk factors we need to be clear-eyed about?

**Format**: Prioritized top-to-bottom by importance. Each DK is a concise bullet with title and brief description. Sub-bullets may include:
- **(a) Mitigants** — offsetting factors that make us more comfortable
- **(b) Tests** — additional analysis or research as next steps to better grasp the risk

No artificial limit on number of DKs. Include what's material.

### 11. Next Steps
What should happen next? Specific actions, questions for management, diligence items to resolve.

</memo_sections>

<writing_style>

## Writing Instructions

- Concise, focused on the essential — no filler, no throat-clearing
- Write like a PE professional explaining a deal to a partner
- Lead with conclusions, then support with evidence
- Page citations throughout when sourcing from CIM: `[p. X]`
- When data is missing: "Not disclosed" — never estimate without labeling it
- 4-5 pages for screening memo, expandable to 8-10 for active diligence
- Every sentence earns its place
- Specific over generic — "dental IT infrastructure" not "specialized services"
- Thesis section should be punchy and specific, not generic "value creation levers"

</writing_style>
